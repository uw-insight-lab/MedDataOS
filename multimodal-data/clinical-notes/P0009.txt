History of Present Illness:

The patient is a 79-year-old male with a history of aortic stenosis
(followed for 3 years, previously graded as moderate), hypertension, and
osteoporosis who presents with two syncopal episodes over the past month,
both occurring during physical exertion. The first episode occurred
while climbing stairs, and the second while walking uphill. In both
cases, he experienced sudden loss of consciousness lasting approximately
10-15 seconds with spontaneous recovery and no post-ictal confusion.

He also reports progressive exertional dyspnea over the past 3-4
months, now occurring with minimal effort such as walking one block on
flat ground. He describes episodes of exertional lightheadedness that
have become more frequent. He denies chest pain at rest, though he
notes a vague substernal pressure with moderate exertion. He denies
palpitations, orthopnea, paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnea, or peripheral
edema.

His medications include amlodipine 5 mg daily, hydrochlorothiazide 12.5
mg daily, alendronate 70 mg weekly, calcium with vitamin D supplement
daily, and acetaminophen as needed for musculoskeletal pain. He avoids
codeine due to prior severe nausea and vomiting. He is a former social
smoker (5 pack-year history, quit 30 years ago) and does not drink
alcohol.

Vitals: temperature 36.6 C, heart rate 68 bpm, respiratory rate 18/min,
blood pressure 134/62 mmHg (narrow pulse pressure noted), SpO2 97% on
room air. Examination reveals a harsh, crescendo-decrescendo systolic
ejection murmur grade 4/6 best heard at the right upper sternal border,
radiating to the carotids bilaterally. Carotid upstrokes are delayed
(pulsus parvus et tardus). S2 is diminished. An S4 gallop is present.
Lungs are clear. No peripheral edema.
